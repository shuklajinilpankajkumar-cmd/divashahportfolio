Case Study

E-Commerce Website
Redesign

StyleLine is a popular fashion e-commerce platform enabling users to browse, wishlist, and purchase clothing effortlessly. The platform was facing usability issues that led to a decline in user satisfaction and engagement. Our goal was to redesign the app, improving its UX/UI to provide a seamless, intuitive, and enjoyable experience — ultimately increasing user retention and satisfaction.

Challenge

The app had a cluttered interface, making it difficult for users to navigate and find essential features. Users were facing issues with the onboarding process, which was affecting new user adoption rates. The app lacked personalization and customization options, making it less engaging and user-friendly.

Results

The redesigned app features a clean, clutter-free interface, making it easier for users to navigate and access essential features.

The improved onboarding process resulted in a 35% increase in new user adoption rates.

The addition of personalization and customization options enhanced user engagement, leading to a 25% increase in user retention rates.

Quick TL;DR of the case study

🔍
The Problem

Shoppers struggled to find products and complete purchases due to a cluttered, hard-to-navigate interface with a poor mobile experience.

✏️
What I Did

I shaped the product direction from research and redesigned core flows — discovery, product detail, and checkout — end-to-end.

💡
Key Idea

Reduce friction at every step so that finding a product and completing a purchase feels effortless — especially on mobile.

Now, let's get into the details

#1 The design challenge

How might we help shoppers navigate StyleLine's catalogue and complete purchases with more confidence, while reducing the frustration caused by a cluttered and unintuitive interface?

#2 Why this problem mattered

This project began with a simple observation: the hardest parts of online shopping are rarely about not knowing what you want — they are about not being able to find it or trust the process enough to buy it.

Shoppers were abandoning carts not because they lost interest, but because they hit friction:

  • → how filters worked (or didn't)
  • → why the final price changed at checkout
  • → where to find their saved items again
  • → what to do when a size was out of stock
  • → whether their order had actually gone through

These were not isolated problems. They formed a larger pattern of distrust and drop-off.

Understanding the context

We grounded the project in both analytics data and lived user experience.

#2 Research approach

We used a mix of interviews, session recordings, and in-context observation to understand where shoppers experienced the most friction.

🎙️

Interviews with both new and returning customers across age groups

📹

Session recordings and heatmaps to identify drop-off points in real flows

🗂️

Affinity diagramming to cluster friction patterns and behavioural themes

🧭

Empathy mapping to understand the emotional drivers behind cart abandonment

Voices from research

A few quotes captured the emotional weight behind these patterns:

"I just give up when I can't find my size quickly. Why is this so hard?"

Isha · Frequent online shopper

"I always have to re-search for items I liked. There's no good wishlist here."

Meera · Grad Student

"The checkout adds fees at the very end with no warning. I've abandoned so many carts."

Rahul · Working professional

These were not just isolated frustrations. They pointed to a system where the experience depended too much on user patience rather than good design.

From research to insight

To move from observations to design decisions, we synthesised findings through affinity mapping and empathy mapping.

This helped us see that the problem was bigger than UI clutter. Shoppers were missing:

🙋 Confidence to trust the checkout 📄 Structure to find products efficiently 👥 Social proof and honest reviews 💪 Control over filters and preferences
Key insight

The biggest gap was not a lack of products — it was the absence of a reliable, frictionless path from discovery to purchase. That shifted the project from "how do we show more items?" to "how do we help people buy with confidence?"

#5 Defining the users

Two distinct user perspectives emerged from research, helping us frame the problem more clearly:

🛍️
The Casual Shopper

Browses for inspiration but drops off when discovery feels overwhelming or when trust in the checkout is low.

🎯
The Intentional Buyer

Knows what they want but gets frustrated when filters are weak, sizes are unclear, or the checkout adds surprise fees.

How might we design a shopping experience that helps users find exactly what they want — fast — and checkout with complete confidence?

Product strategy and prioritisation

#6 Ideation

During ideation we explored a wide set of possibilities:

🏷️ Smart Filters 🛍️ Wishlist Overhaul 📸 Visual Search 💳 One-Page Checkout 📦 Order Tracking Feed 🌐 Outfit Builder

To narrow the solution, we prioritised ideas using three questions:

#1

Does this solve a high-frequency shopper pain point?

#2

Does this reduce friction, not just add a new feature?

#3

Can this realistically ship within a connected product experience?

The Product Vision

A single, trustworthy shopping experience where users can discover, save, and buy — without friction, surprise, or confusion.

Feature deep dive

#8 Final Design Highlights — each feature addresses a core challenge identified in research.

Smart Discovery

I redesigned the homepage into a personalised feed with visual category tiles, a persistent smart search bar, and fast-apply filters — turning the overwhelming catalogue into an enjoyable discovery experience.

Product Detail Page

I redesigned the PDP with a sticky size selector, 360° image carousel, honest size guide, and social proof (verified reviews + outfit pairings) front and centre — reducing uncertainty before purchase.

One-Page Checkout

We collapsed a 5-step checkout into a single scrollable page with auto-fill, transparent fee breakdown, and a persistent order summary — making the final step feel trustworthy and fast.

Wishlist & Saved Items

I designed a persistent, shareable wishlist with back-in-stock alerts and price-drop notifications — giving shoppers a reason to return and a clear path back to items they loved.

What we tested

#9 Usability Testing — once the prototype was ready, we conducted think-aloud sessions with both casual shoppers and intentional buyers.

  • Could users find products without guidance?
  • Did the checkout feel transparent and trustworthy?
  • Were high-frequency tasks (filter, save, buy) easy to complete?
  • Did the product feel reliable and clear?

What we found

Testing showed the concept was valuable, but interaction details needed work.

🤔 Hidden interactions

The wishlist save action was not obvious enough. Several users overlooked the heart icon and were unsure how to save items.

⚠️ Weak system feedback

Users were unsure whether items had been saved or if their cart had updated, because the interface lacked clear micro-confirmations.

📋 Filter complexity

For first-time users, too many filter options created decision paralysis. A simplified "quick filter" bar was added in iterations.

#9 Iterations based on feedback

Improved clarity and reduced friction through better visibility, clearer feedback states, simplified filters, and task shortcuts.

✨

By the end of the project, StyleLine had evolved from an overwhelming catalogue into a focused, enjoyable shopping experience. Observed impact includes:

👥

↓ reduced cart abandonment across all device types

📈

↑ increased confidence in completing purchases

👍

↑ positive emotional response to the redesigned checkout flow

What I Learned

1) Clutter is a prioritisation problem, not just a visual one.
2) Mobile-first is a forcing function for clarity — not a constraint.
3) Small moments of feedback (confirmations, animations, clear states) have an outsized impact on trust.

Given more time, I would focus on improving AI-powered size recommendations, adding trust and social proof features, and personalising the onboarding flow to make the product more scalable and reliable.
