UX CASE STUDY

Belong Here

A relocation support platform that helps newcomers make informed decisions, navigate unfamiliar systems, and build a sense of belonging in a new place.

Students and early-career professionals face more than logistics when they relocate — they face uncertainty, unfamiliarity, and the challenge of starting fresh. Belong Here brings together trusted guidance, real local experiences, and a supportive community to make the transition smoother, simpler and more human.

Research approach

We used a mixed-method research approach to understand relocation from multiple angles, lived experiences, broader patterns in the literature, and synthesis methods that helped us translate insights into design opportunities.

Affinity map
Empathy map

These artifacts helped us move from raw observations to clearer themes around uncertainty, trust, emotional adjustment, and everyday decision-making.

Primary research

Interviews

We spoke with people who had recently relocated to understand both practical and emotional challenges during transition.

Secondary research

Academic + grey literature

We reviewed existing literature to validate broader patterns around uncertainty, adjustment, belonging, and fragmented support.

Synthesis method

Affinity mapping

We clustered recurring observations into themes such as information overload, housing stress, emotional adjustment, and daily-life confusion.

Synthesis method

Empathy mapping

We synthesized what users said, thought, felt, and did to better understand motivations, concerns, and coping behaviors.

This mixed-method approach helped us understand not just what newcomers struggle with, but why those struggles build over time and where support systems currently break down.

What we learned

Research and testing revealed clear patterns that shaped the direction of our solution.

Hyperlocal context mattered

Users needed neighborhood-level, lived-in guidance they could actually act on.

Support needed to stay social

Relocation questions were ongoing, so support had to extend beyond one-time answers.

Trust needed interface cues

Advice was only useful if users could judge who it was coming from.

Information had to become action

The system needed to help users move from uncertainty to decision-making.

One insight became especially important:

Key insight

The biggest gap was not access to information, but access to support that felt reliable, local, and easy to act on. Advice already existed, but it was fragmented, hard to verify, and difficult to use in real decisions.

As we synthesized the research, two roles began to shape the system more clearly:
the person seeking support, and the person whose lived experience could make that support meaningful.

The Newcomer

A newcomer making everyday decisions without enough local context, trusted guidance, or confidence.

The Guide

Someone whose lived experience can support others, if the system makes that knowledge visible, credible, and reusable.

Design opportunity

Our research pointed to a clearer opportunity:

How might we turn fragmented relocation advice into a structured system of hyperlocal guidance, community support, and trustworthy decision-making tools?

The goal was not just to give users more information,
but to make support more structured, credible, and usable in real decisions.

Solution overview

The final concept brought together four core experiences:

Community Feed

A central place to ask questions, share practical advice, and browse relocation knowledge from people with lived local experience.

VR Maps

A hyperlocal exploration layer that helps newcomers understand areas, places, and everyday environments through contextual guidance.

Chats & Groups

A conversational support space for follow-up questions, peer discussion, and more ongoing forms of help.

Profiles & Trust Signals

A credibility layer that makes advice easier to evaluate through visible activity, identity, and community participation.

Each part of the system supported the same goal: helping newcomers move from scattered advice to confident, real-world decisions.

We wanted the experience to feel like one connected support system, not a collection of unrelated tools. So the product was organized around recurring relocation needs: discovering local insight, asking questions, getting direct support, and building trust over time.

Navigation structure: Feed → VR Maps → Chats → Notifications / Profile

This made the product easier to understand at a glance and helped users move between exploration, conversation, and action without losing context.

Core user flows

These flows show how Belong Here turns relocation support into something more guided, local, and actionable.

Personalized onboarding

Newcomers begin by choosing their destination, timeline, and priorities so the app can tailor support from the very start.

Asking for help in the feed

Exploring through VR Maps

Place-based notes help users learn from local insight in context, turning scattered city knowledge into navigable guidance.

Following up through chats

Support continues in groups and direct conversations, helping users move from one-time answers to ongoing peer guidance.

Feature Deep Dive

Each feature translated a key research insight into a more structured and actionable form of support.

Personalized onboarding

Different users entered the system with different needs.

Problem

Newcomers arrive with different timelines, priorities, and support needs, so generic onboarding would feel broad and irrelevant.

Design response

We designed a role-based onboarding flow for both newcomers and verified locals. Newcomers personalize destination, timeline, and priorities; locals verify identity and expertise.

Community feed

Everyday questions needed a more visible and reusable support space.

Problem

People already had questions, but asking for help often depended on scattered groups, repeated one-to-one support, or informal advice that was hard to revisit later.

Design response

We designed a shared community feed where users can browse local questions, ask for help, add tags or media, and post anonymously when needed. This made practical relocation support easier to ask for, discover, and reuse across the community.

Key takeaway

The feed turned private dependence into visible, reusable support.

Testing the prototype

Once the prototype was ready, we evaluated it through a combination of Think-Aloud usability testing and expert review to understand how confidently users could complete key flows and where the interface created hesitation or confusion.

What we wanted to learn

  • Could users find key features without guidance?
  • Were core tasks easy to complete?
  • Where did the interface create hesitation or confusion?
  • Did the product feel clear, credible and trustworthy?

Approach

Mixed-method evaluation combining user testing and expert review.

User testing method

4 Think aloud testing

Participants verbalized their thoughts while completing predefined tasks.

Expert testing method

4 Expert testing

Experts used task error recording sheets followed by a post-task questionnaire.

Metrics used

  • Task completion
  • Hesitation / confusion points
  • Error patterns
  • Difficulty ratings
  • Severity and user impact noted by experts

3 core tasks tested

Across both user and expert evaluations, participants completed the same three core tasks.

01

Create a new post

Users created a new post on the home feed with title, description and tags.

02

Explore the VR map around a selected restaurant

Users located "Weber Grill Restaurant" and explored the surrounding area in VR.

03

Send a message in a community group

Users sent a message in the "Downtown Living Tips" group within the chat interface.

Key findings

01

VR map needed stronger orientation cues

Both users and experts struggled with direction, movement, and spatial understanding in the VR map. Lack of orientation cues and clear navigation controls made users feel disoriented and uncertain.

02

Posting needed clearer action cues

Users could create posts successfully, but expected the post action to be more prominent and more aligned with familiar posting patterns. Multi-tag selection and post-submission feedback also caused hesitation.

03

Messaging felt immediately intuitive

The chat flow was completed smoothly by both users and experts with minimal hesitation. The interaction aligned well with existing messaging mental models.
